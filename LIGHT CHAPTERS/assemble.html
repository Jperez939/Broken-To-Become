Assemble

Where does the picture end? What’s inside the picture; what’s outside? What does that boundary zone between what’s in and out look like? Is there meaning in that middle?


Justice reflects on collage not as a static image, but as an act of questioning and constructing. He explains that collage-making interrupts the illusion of a single, seamless frame and replaces it with an awareness of how images are made, torn, and rearranged.

The digital realm, he argues, doesn’t eliminate this process amplifies it, making assembling faster and more recursive.

Every act of layering becomes a negotiation between what’s kept and what’s erased.

He explains how digital montage transforms collage from a single plane of assembly into a space of multiplicity.

Layers in Photoshop or any editing tool are not merely stacked—they communicate, interact, and create hybrid meanings. He likens digital montage to “thinking in multiple frames,” a method of holding contradictions together.

This reflects the tension between brokenness and reconstruction—exactly your theme of shards reassembled into coherence.

“To work deliberately in the form of the fragment can be seen as stopping or appearing to stop a work closer, in the process, to what Blanchot would call the origin of writing, the center rather than the sphere. It may be seen as a formal integration, an integration into the form itself, of a question about the process of writing..."
